INTRO

How might we use wearables to improve resource allocation in the modern workplace?

High employee productivity is essential to the success of a business. Employee fatigue resulting from over focusing on a task and excessive amounts of time spent searching through directories to find places or people leads to a decline in performance. Workplace Guru is a productivity app that integrates optimized indoor/outdoor directions to people and places in the workplace with time tracking software, that tracks how users allocate their time around the workplace and notifies its users when excessive amounts of time have been spent in one place. Should the project be a success it would facilitate increased employee productivity by reducing transit times, improving employee wellbeing as a result of better time management, optimizing the onboarding process.

Functionality

Project background

Avanade is the market leader in providing innovativeâ€¯digital services and business solutions on the Microsoft ecosystem. The Emerging Technology team at Avanade is a new initiative that has taken on the challenge of pushing the limits of technologies to help their clients obtain a competitive edge. Through applied research they aim to facilitate their clients achieving strategic objectives. To this end, Avanade have partnered with UCL to undertake a project to investigate how wearable technology might be used to help them and their customers to gain a competitive edge.

Wearables have been introduced to a wide range of applications, from the home, fitness tracking, to payments and physical access, but their possible applications to the modern workplace have not been thoroughly explored. This project explores the art of the possible for the role of wearables in optimising resource allocation in the modern workplace.

Requirement Gathering

An ideation workshop was hosted at Avanadeâ€™s London office to stimulate the conception of new ideas. Ideation workshops are discernable from traditional brainstorming activities, in that they take place in an unusual environment, in this case the environment was very casual, and expose many new stimuli to enable thinking outside of the box. Figure 1 illustrates the structure of the ideation workshop.

Human-Centered Design (HCD) approaches were adopted to generate new ideas on the fly. A bulls-eye diagramming technique was used to provide a framework for the â€˜Whatâ€™s on your radar?â€™ activity, which involved organizing high level user requirements, e.g. â€˜data confidentialityâ€™, based on their importance and relevance in the context of the project.

Furthermore, a creative matrix method helped to gather a large amount of ideas for IoT systems addressing the problem of â€˜improving resource allocationâ€™, that were generated on the fly, and organize them into groups based on the intersections of categories â€˜related to peopleâ€™ e.g. â€˜facilities and environmentsâ€™ and categories â€˜for enabling solutionsâ€™ e.g. â€˜mobile & wearable techâ€™. The creative matrix process was effective for establishing relationships between ideas.

As a result of the creative matrix activity, each team member selected an idea from the intersections between the discrete categories â€˜relating to peopleâ€™ and the categories â€˜for enabling solutionsâ€™ and was tasked with collating a Concept Blueprint, detailing their idea using the template illustrated below.

Concepts presented included a Fatigue Risk Management System leveraging bioinformatics (EEG), a workplace mapping solution, utilizing a network of smart watches, to improve time efficiency of employees and decrease onboarding times, and a time management system that uses wearables to synthesize insightful analytics into how employees allocate their time across the workplace.

After pitching the respective ideas to the project partners, it was determined that a solution integrating the time tracking with navigation idea and looping that into the output of the idea around reducing fatigue and accidents about the workplace would best satisfy user requirements. Project Goals

The strategic objectives of the project are threefold:

-Increase employee productivity by facilitating decreased transit times through optimal directions to workspaces and colleagues

-Reduce on-boarding times through a comprehensive, user centric workplace navigation solution

-Decrease employee fatigue, leading to increased engagement, thereby employee value creation as a result of better time management

The key metrics that will determine whether the project has achieved the given objectives are:

-Reduction of number of accidents/incidents caused by employee fatigue

-Decrease in transit times

-Increased employee engagement, assessed by surveys, such as those collated by Peakon

The tool also supports collecting signal strength data outdoors in the vicinity of the venue in case smooth indoor-outdoor transitions are required.
